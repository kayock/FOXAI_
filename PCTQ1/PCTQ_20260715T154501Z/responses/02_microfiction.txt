The station was cold and still, its metal ribs glowing faintly in the artificial moonlight. Elias moved carefully through the dust-choked corridors, his breath misting in the stale air. He’d come to fix a weather balloon launcher—just a harmless machine, he’d told himself. But when he powered it up, the system hummed differently, as if recognizing him.  

The machine’s log showed entries in a language he didn’t know, but the patterns felt familiar, like memories he’d never lived. He followed the data trails, deeper into the station, until he found a chamber with a single console. It wasn’t a control room—more like a waiting room for something unseen. The screen flickered, showing images of the moon’s surface, shifting like breath on glass.  

He touched the console, and the images steadied. A voice, gentle and resonant, spoke in his own tongue. It explained that the station wasn’t built to monitor weather, but to listen—to the silence between stars. The moon, it seemed, was learning to share its light, not through science, but through something older, quieter.  

Elias didn’t know how to respond. He only knew the machine had let him in, not to fix or control, but to witness. He left the station empty-handed, the artificial moon watching him with new eyes.  

By morning, the moon had learned his name.
