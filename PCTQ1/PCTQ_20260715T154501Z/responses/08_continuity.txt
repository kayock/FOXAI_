The water was thick with memory, each ripple distorting the quiet reflections of books that had once whispered secrets. Mira sat at the bow, her grip steady on the oar, while Jon drifted in the seat behind, his broken compass spinning wildly in its case. The blue key she carried pressed against her palm, its edges cool and unyielding.

They moved through the library as if navigating a forgotten language, the flooded shelves bending under the weight of time. Mira’s eyes flicked to the green light that flickered near the library’s entrance, its glow pulsing like a heartbeat lost in the dark. She didn’t speak of it, just kept her gaze fixed ahead, the boat gliding between floating tomes and broken desks.

Jon’s compass had long stopped pointing north. Instead, it turned toward the regrets he’d gathered like stones in his pockets—moments he’d wish he’d said something, choices that had left him stranded. He traced the worn edges of the device, wondering if the map they sought was even real, or if it was a trick of the mind to keep searching.

The paper birds they’d found earlier fluttered in the boat’s wake, their delicate wings catching glimmers of the green light. Mira had tucked one into her sleeve, its folded shape a quiet reminder of something she’d yet to share. It was Jon who noticed the distant bells now, faint but persistent, echoing through the flooded halls like a forgotten melody.

He asked nothing of her, but the silence between them felt heavy, thick with unspoken things. The boat drifted on, the water swallowing their words. They searched the shelves, their hands brushing past titles that no longer existed, hoping to find a map that might not be there. The compass whirred, spinning, as if trying to make sense of a world that had lost its direction.

When they reached the far side of the library, the water still held its breath. The green light dimmed, and the bells grew quieter, retreating into the distance. Mira stood, her feet sinking into the silt as she reached for the key. Jon watched her, his hands on the oar, waiting for the explanation that never came.

Neither of them looked back.
