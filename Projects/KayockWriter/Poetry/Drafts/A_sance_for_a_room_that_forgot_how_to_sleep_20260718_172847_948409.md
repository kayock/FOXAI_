# A séance for a room that forgot how to sleep

The couch is cold, the TV's dead,
But I'm still spinning, still the one who holds the thread.
This ain't a bedtime story for a kid who needs a nap,
This is a séance for a room that forgot how to sleep.
I'm sitting here in my boxers, the light from the streetlamp
dust-bumping through the window like a ghost on a wire,
telling you the truth that my mouth forgot to say when I was young.
Remember the winter when the heater hissed like a dying cat?
I was six, holding a half-eaten apple, watching the ice
form a cage on the glass door, thinking that if I just
squeezed hard enough, maybe the cold would crack and let the sun in.
Instead, the door stayed shut, and the world stayed outside.
They told me stories then, too, but these were the ones
that fit the walls of my house, the ones that made the shadows
look like friends who never left.
"The bad stuff waits," they said, "but the good stuff stays."
I believed them until I was old enough to see that
good stuff is a loan, and bad stuff is the only thing
you can keep forever, locked in a safe made of concrete.
Now the air is thin, the room is too quiet for words,
so I'm screaming the history out, trying to wake up
the empty chairs where my brothers and sisters used to sit.
I'm telling you how I learned to fold my feet to fit the space,
how I learned to speak in whispers just to avoid the noise,
how I learned that loyalty isn't about standing tall,
it's about not letting the one you love fall, even when
they're the only ones standing.
I saw the injustice written in the rust on the fence,
in the way the mailman dropped the letter and didn't look back,
in the way the city turned a corner and forgot the name
of the block where I cried for help and nobody came.
But here's the thing, the twist that keeps the story alive:
even when the world is a graveyard and I'm the only one left standing,
I still tell the tales.
Because if I stop, who will remember the heat of the sun
on a back porch, the taste of a cookie that wasn't mine but tasted
like home? Who will know that we survived the winter by
just being alive, by making a home out of a broken place?
So listen close, you empty room, you silent street,
I'm finishing the verse, locking the door on the past,
and walking out into the future that's still waiting,
still breathing, still hoping that tomorrow
might be a little less cold than today.
