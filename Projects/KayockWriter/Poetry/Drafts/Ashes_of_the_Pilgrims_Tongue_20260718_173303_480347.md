# Ashes of the Pilgrims' Tongue

We carved our names into the ribs of abandoned spaceships,  
where silence held more stories than words ever could.  
The air tasted like rust and resolve,  
and every breath was a pact with the ghosts of what we’d leave behind.  

I learned to speak in salvaged parts—  
a radio’s static, a rusted lock’s whisper,  
the way shadows cling to a man’s back  
when he’s carried too many weights.  

They called us pilgrims of the hollowed sky,  
travelers stitching broken constellations into new maps.  
But the stars remembered—we were just their next flicker.  

We ate salt and hunger, shared stories  
that left teeth marks on the air.  
No one asked how the meal tasted,  
only if we’d seen fire in the dark.  

When the ship’s heart gave way,  
we didn’t mourn. We just lit the fuse  
and watched the sky write our epitaphs in ash.  

Now I stand where the path ends,  
telling the world what it never asked to hear—  
about the hands that built, the hands that broke,  
and the hands that still reach for something more.  

The wind carries my voice,  
but the world doesn’t turn to listen.  
And that’s okay.  
The story isn’t done yet.
