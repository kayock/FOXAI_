# Stains and silent invitations

The wind in Pueblo cuts like a blade through the old clothes, and I remember my grandmother’s hands, always busy mending holes in rags, whispering, *“We’re not poor—we’re just not dressed for the party.”* But here, in this moment, the world wears its wealth like a scent—cinnamon and leather—while I smell dust and laundry detergent gone cold.

There’s a man outside the diner, sitting on a stool with a coffee that’s long gone cold. His shirt is threadbare, his pants held up by a belt that’s seen better days. He stares at the window, watching people laugh and eat like they’ve never known hunger. I wonder if he’s ever felt the weight of being invisible, like a ghost in a world that only notices the ghosts with money.

The diner is loud with laughter, the smell of grease and hope. I order a burger, but my stomach growls anyway. I think of the times I’ve tried to fit in, to pretend I belong here, where clothes are armor. My pockets are heavy with change, but I can’t shake the feeling that even if I had a suit, I’d still be the one standing behind the door, waiting to be let in.

I walk past the man again, my shoes scuffing the floor. He nods once, and I wonder if he sees me the way I see him—someone carrying a load no one else notices. The sun slips lower, casting long shadows across the street. It’s not the kind of light that warms, just the kind that tells stories.

Back home, my room is small, the walls lined with photos of places I’ve never been. I trace the edges of the ones I’ve taken, trying to feel closer to them. My phone buzzes—a message from my cousin, asking if I can meet him at the diner. I reply, *“Sure,”* but I don’t tell him about the man outside, or the weight in my chest when I think of the world’s sharp edges.

Later, sitting across from my cousin, I notice his watch—expensive, new. He smiles, but I don’t smile back. We talk about everything but the unspoken things—the hunger, the clothes, the way the world keeps moving while we’re left behind. It’s not that we’re poor; it’s that we’re not dressed for the party, and the invitations never come to us.

As I walk home, the wind cuts sharper, carrying with it the scent of rain. I think of my grandmother’s hands, still mending, still whispering. Maybe there’s a story here, a truth hidden in the stains and the silence. I don’t know what it is yet, but I feel it in my bones. And for now, that’s enough.
