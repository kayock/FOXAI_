# When a man loves a woman

When a man loves a woman  

Certainly, I would give my life for her—no one would dispute that fact. After all, she was a goddess with hair strung by angels and eyes that twinkled like black diamonds on a face that would even make God weep. For all those reasons and a million more, that is why I did what I did.  

Some called me crazy. Others called me obsessed. But I always knew it was love—strange, reckless, undeniable love. The myth, the legend, the tale that love could conquer all.  

It was a cold spring night when I met her. When our eyes met, I felt them dance, tugging at my soul. To her, I was ghetto trash, but I felt our kindred spirits brushing. Then, on April 16th, an Easter Sunday, when she first stepped into my arms, my heart seized her, and I knew I would never let her go. And never did.  

One year and two months later, it ended—badly. Though I loved her more with every day. I was broken. When I saw her in the arms of another, I screamed, and so did my heart.  

So I went on, like time does—slowly, thoughtfully. Though I loved her truly, I tried to move on. Even with many women, only a few had touched my heart. And those who did hurt me worse than the one before.  

Now I’m 25, still madly in love with her. Even though she is happy, I’m not. For seven years, I carried my secret burden, wishing she were there. Never once to see her. I told everyone I was over her, that she was water under a bridge.  

A lie. As big as any spoken. But they were all fooled. Often, I’d look at the few pictures of her I had, marveling at my own foolishness. Sometimes I’d dream the past was repaired, and every morning, no one cared.  

My life went on like this. No one noticed the spark had left our eyes. Occasionally, I’d go out. Drinking wasn’t my thing, but dancing, new connections—those made it worthwhile.  

Chile’s was the club. An average American meat market. The urban, techno beats matched the custom light show.  

The crowded club was hard to navigate, let alone dance. I stood on a rise above the floor, scanning, when I saw her.  

She was even lovelier, her strength radiating from her movements, her seductiveness undeniable. And if it were possible for me not to notice him, I wouldn’t have. But why fight the impossible?  

He was awkward, I’d say impish if it weren’t so cruel. I was amazed. Purposefully, her eyes rose, meeting mine. I saw it—those eyes, dancing again, just like our first encounter. Quickly, bitterness and loss replaced the electricity, the recognition. Painfully, I broke our gaze, pretending to study a menu.  

The DJ announced last call, last song. I turned, but a crowd surged where they’d been.  

Softly, I sighed, ensuring no one heard. Numbly, I moved toward the door as a crowd gathered, spilling into the parking lot. Normally, I’d say, “Fuck it,” and go home. But something was different, calling to me. Of course, I went.  

In the center of the crowd, my nightmare began. They stood together—her, him. She was crying, mascara streaking down her face like two ebony rivers. Though he’d fought, he was beaten. One eye swollen shut, the other nearly there. His thick eyebrows, what was left of them, resembled gizzards. He clutched his arm, injured, muttering something I couldn’t catch.  

You’d think this would bring me pleasure. It didn’t. Anything that makes her unhappy wounds me. So I moved toward them. As I neared, I saw he was worse than I’d thought.  

Between coughs, he muttered about “one guy.” She stroked his hair, motherly, and at that moment, I knew I’d never have a chance. I probably would’ve broken down then, but I saw it out of the corner of my eye.  

A smaller man, a lemon-sized welt on his right eye. The moment I saw the tiny chrome revolver, I knew what it was. “Motherfucker,” I assume he said, then squeezed two shots at her and her new husband.  

I’d like to say I thought of saving him, of being a hero. I can’t. If there’d been time to think, it’d have been for her. But there was no time. So I lunged, taking it.  

The slugs didn’t register as they hit. My leather coat must’ve taken most. The first thing I felt was my shoulder cracking against the concrete garden. Then, an odd cracking in… of all things, my chest.  

As the bullets tore through my ribcage, deflating my lung, one lodging in my left ventricle, I gasped. Painfully, my chest warmed with blood. I heard a hissing, a strange, familiar sound—like a balloon losing its breath.  

The sensation of blood rising up my throat was pleasurable, almost ecstatic. Warm, with a cooling voice inviting darkness like a warm blanket. Instinctively, I knew it was almost over.  

Yet, still, I thought she’d see. But she was gone, pushed back by the chaos. Strangely, I didn’t think of God or heaven. I thought of two things: that I finally played a role in making her happy, and how I wished I could see those lovely dancing eyes once more.  

Then, suddenly, the world was gone. I was stuck here, telling my story to no one. But at least she’s happy.”  

The end.
