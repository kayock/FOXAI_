# The Weaver's Tapestry: Echoes of Leo

**I. The Accident**

The universe did not end with a scream, but with a kaleidoscope. It was a violent, beautiful explosion of color and sound, the epitome of happiness and the total loss of self. I was taking my usual morning walk when four drunk children, much too young and too rowdy, decided to test their courage. They hit me. Not once did they slow down until three days later, when the police finally arrived with their flashlights.

My hip was broken in sixteen places. My skull leaked its awful brain matter onto the pavement of the city street. Dana was devastated, but I didn't know that, for I was already gone, a ghost haunting the margins of her grief. The first thing I saw was everything. The universe danced visually, drowning out the pain of the impact.

Then, a voice cut through the silence, a sound that nothing could ever describe.
"What are you?" it asked.

"I'm dead," I said, not even trying to hide the agony in my voice. "And I am in pain. Dana... we were trying... to have a baby. We wanted a boy. She insisted he be called Leo. Leo Alderon Jr. My junior, but now... I am nothing."

"Again," the voice repeated. "What are you?"

Wailing, I cried out for my Dana. "I'm a husband. Not the best, probably not even very good..."

"Again," it said. "What are you?"

Falling to my knees, I answered the truth. "I... I'm a writer."

**II. The Office of Memory**

Suddenly, the slam of a door assaulted the world. I found myself in my first office. It was a pantry built onto the mobile home Dana and I had lived in when we first married. On the small fake-oak desk sat my old Epson typewriter next to a monstrous stack of blank sheets. To the right was the stained-glass nameplate I'd made in eighth grade. The white letters of my name gleamed from the desk lamp.

With no thought, I opened the only drawer and found the picture of Dana I always saved. She was sitting down, one hand on her chin, one leg up. Her long, wavy hair hung over the left side of her torso.

Shaking uncontrollably, I fell to my knees, head down and solemn. Squeezing my fists, I scrambled as loud as I could. "Dana!"

The echoes that surrounded me were alien screams of pain. They filled me, assaulting me, trying to destroy me with their regret. Hidden inside of these, I heard her. Her musical voice was just a whisper, but it was there. Tasting the saltiness of my tears, I turned and saw the hollow door that served my office. On shaky legs, I stood up. The deep brownness of the door scared me just as much as the cold metal of the knob. Still, I turned the brass knob and opened the door. It opened upon a whirlwind that scooped me up into it with no time for thought. The storm was made of memories, emotions, and the situations that always devoured men with no opportunity for denial. In it, I knew she was trapped, being kept from me against her wishes. With all the power of my will, I tried to see her, but the memories of time were too strong.

**III. The Cat and The Game**

Once, as a child, my friend Tommy killed a cat. The cat was huge, the size of a big poodle. Tommy and I were walking to school as we did on most mornings, quiet from sleep and dreading the day of learning. Cutting through the prairies as always, we heard a loud and angry rear.

I stopped, a little scared but mostly curious. Tommy, however, kept going as if the sound never even resonated within his ears. I could hear the crash of the grass as the feline jumped towards Tommy's face, attaching itself to his lower lips with claws that looked diseased and dirty. Tommy began to howl first in surprise, then in pure horror. He slapped at the mad thing as he jumped around in an almost comic dance; the tenacious cat never the less held on like grim death. A small winding began to escape from Tom's throat. To my young years, it sounded like the wind that sometimes awoke me with a fright.

Broken from my shock, I yelled, "Tom, drop and roll!" But already his glasses were off and his red eyes looked like they were ready to kill. With the resolve of the man he would grow to be, he grabbed the cat under its legs and pulled. With the ripping of his own flesh, the cat went to the ground, where Tom, oblivious of the eight slits of gouged flesh and gushing blood, continued to stomp its head. At least a hundred times did his legs come down as the cries, tears, and blood increased with each one. After the cat was a bloody ball of flesh and meat, Tommy stopped. Still crying, he turned to me and took his cracked glasses from my outstretched hand. Though I wanted to take him back home, he insisted on going to school with me. Once there, he was sent straight to the emergency room.

Years later, as a teenager, my buddy Chase and I ditched school to go catch a game of ball. The Ratliffe Ravens were the number one minor league team, which just happened to be our local team. Though no contest for the major leagues, we were quite good. So, us in our infinite wisdom, decided that skipping school was justified by the eight-one stats we had accumulated this year. The game was actually quite good. The Ravens, pitching a no-hitter, won 8-0. The pops and candy bars were actually some of the best I ever had. All in all, it was one of the greatest times in my young life. On the way home, we met some girls. I got a pretty nasty sunburn. Though it would hurt a whole hell of a lot later, for that time, all was right with the world.

**IV. The Meeting of Dana**

With a slight pain, I began to relive the lonely life I had as a young adult. Addicted to slightly more than a Bud Light in a bottle or reefer, the downward spirals of my life continued to deepen. Though, now I would like to say I was trying. I do know better.

I was working at one of the nameless telemarketing firms that sold things from books to anti-aging creams. Yes, I was good at this, but that says nothing since I was good at everything I actually tried. Yet still, I had no drive; my ambition was being devoured by whatever I put up my nose or was smoking at the time. For nearly six years, I went on like this. Being so fucked up that only my telephone job was actually feasible. Then, around the time I actually began to look at myself, I met her.

The first time I saw her, I was so deeply attracted I had to disconnect on the useless call I was taking. Her stroll was luxurious, like some queen within her own court. Her long hair glided about her leather coat. Yes, it was love at first sight, but it was also cautious. Following close behind her was a guy. Though big and around her age (which was six years below me), he just couldn't be her man. I was positive about that. He possessed a kind face and eyes but absolutely none of that savoir-faire that she would require.

To the chair on my left, she sat. Instantly, the sweet aroma of her perfume made its way into my brain, firing off synapses that I would always associate with perfection. Her hands, though small, had slender fingers which ended in the most spectacular of nails. Not because of any shape or sharpness, but because of the intricate peacocks drawn upon them. Each nail was a different interpretation of a sunset that surrounded the bird.

With my heart pounding and my palms sweating, I reached out and touched one. At the same time, I spoke. "Nice, very nice."

She turned, not pulling her hands from me. "Thank you." She said just before meeting my eyes. When our eyes locked, it was as if I got punched in the chest. From the look of her enlarging eyes (which were so brown and so piercing) and her quick chest movements, I think the feeling was mutual. "Hi," she said little sheepishly. "I'm Dana." With a short pause, she continued while looking into my eyes. "And this is my friend Al."

The chubby guy said Hi to me as my mind screamed with delight. "It's such a pleasure to me, you," I said. "My name is Leo."
