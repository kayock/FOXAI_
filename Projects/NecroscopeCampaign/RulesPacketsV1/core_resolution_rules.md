                                     current  subject, precisely defines the ability ofthe cyborg.
                                        A cyborg   can  link himself to a computer  or other electronic system of circuits
                                     without the use of a physical medium  (keyboard,   mouse,  touch-screen,  buttons,
                                     joystick, etc.). He needs only  to get near  the computer  or disk he wishes to read.
                                     Then his mind  and  talent take over. At that point he can  psychically "read" the
                                     binary   code that is the basis of every computer  program.
                                         Since a  binary    system contains   only   two  options,  electronic devices have
                                     circuits with two possible states: "on"  and  "off." Contrary   to the belief of psuedo-
                                     intellectual computer  experts, the on  state is designated by  a certain, pre-defined
                                     voltage running   through the circuit, while the off state is designated by  a lower
                                     voltage, not  the complete absence   of current.  The cyborg   appears  to have  the
                                     ability to detect and  distinguish between  these two levels of voltage, and  hence
                                     determine the state of a particular circuit in a chip or sector of a hard disk, floppy
                                     diskette, or tape drive.
                                         But knowledge  of binary   data  means   nothing  to someone   who  cannot    read
                                     computer   code, just like a  string of Sanskrit rvords looks like gibberish to a
                                     French-speaking  person. Most cyborgs  have to learn to read machine   language,
                                     and  to translate between  hexadecimal  and  alphanumeric    systems. Even though
                                     you   might be able to realize that a group  of binary   digits represents the number
                                     65, you   need to know   that that value signifies the capital letter "A" in ASCII code.
                                     Only by  going through the two stages of identification can  a cyborg   actually  read
                                     the data stored in the computer  system without the help ofthe computer's  system
                                     software.
                                         What  makes  the cyborg's   job  even harder is the proliferation of computer
                                     hardware   with different architectures. Unless  you   know   the order of binary
                                     digits, you   may   inadvertantly  group  those bits into bytes in reverse order. To
                                     reduce the risk of errant translation  of a given computer   system, most cyborgs
                                     memorize  as many    architecture types as possible, and  after a while, learn to
                                     quickly determine the structure of a particular system.
                                         Cyborgs  are still rare in the world. The frrst cyborg   to become   aware   of his
                                     talent appeared  in the early'60s.  As technology   progresses and  becomes   even
                                     more widespread, the number    of cyborgs  should continue   to rise. Of course,  most
                                     cyborgs  come  from the computer  programmer   and  system designer professions,
                                     but only  because  they recognize the patterns that form in their minds as a binary
